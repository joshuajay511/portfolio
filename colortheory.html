What is HSL

People familiar with image editing programs might be more familiar with HSL than they realize. The main advantage of HSL is that it makes it easy to select a color quickly – otherwise, users would have to painstakingly adjust and tweak RGB sliders until they get the color just right.

Hue

In HSL, the Hue determines what color of the rainbow something is. It’s represented in 360 degrees, like a traditional color wheel. One of the main advantages of HSL over RGB color is that complementary colors are located across from one another, which makes the whole system very intuitive.

Saturation

The distance from the middle of the color wheel is called the ‘Saturation’, or how much of a given hue is present. Looking closely at the color wheel above, it is apparent that the color becomes brighter and more vivid as one travels from the center of the circle to the edge.

Lightness

The ‘Lightness’ of a color is a gradient between black and white, where the ‘bottom’ of the cylinder is a total black, and the ‘top’ of the cylinder is totally white.

Easy Color Paletts

While readability is a big benefit, the most significant advantage is how easy HSL makes picking colors. When working with HEX codes, it’s hard to adjust a color and have it be the same hue but with different saturation or lightness. However, this becomes trivial with HSL, and you can generate all the tints and shades of one hue you could ever desire in seconds.

Jewel Tones

These colors that are richly saturated hues named for gems including sapphire blue, ruby red, amethyst purple, citrine yellow, and emerald green. Think of the deep richness of a red ruby necklace or a royal purple crown. These colors are regal, deep and impart a sense of luxury.


Jewel Tones pair well with Pastel Tones, try combining the two.

Pastels

Pastel colors belong to the pale family of colors. Pink, mauve, and baby blue are commonly used pastel colors, as well as colors like magic mint, peach, periwinkle, and lavender. The colors of this family are usually described as "soothing"


Pastel tones are the most flexible of the collor paletts and work well when combined with Jewels, Earths, and Shades.

Earth Tones

These are colors commonly found in nature. Many earth tones originate from clay pigments like umbre, ochre and sienna. They can be created by combining a pure hue with white, black, or gray. These are considered in the more broad sense a neutral color. They are influenced by the tones of trees, forest, seas and sky and are muted and flat to emulate natural colors.


Earth tones pair best with Pastels or Neuturals

Neuturals

Pure neutral colors include black, white, beige and all grays while near neutrals include browns, tans, and darker colors. These are create by desaturating colors. They can be paired with any of the above categories to create balance.


Neuturals work best when combined with Earth Tones

Fluorescent / Neon

Fluorescence is a result of photoluminescence. Phosphorescent color emits light when excited by visible or ultraviolet light. In the physical world, this is created by neon tubing or ultraviolet reactant paint colors, but in the digital world we can get this same effect by applying very bright and highly saturated colors to our designs.


These colors pop off the page when combine with shade

Shades

The two main shades are black and white (which are not normally considered colors but rather the absence of light or dark or the addition of pure shade or pure tint), other shades also include varying degrees of gray.


Shades work well with Fluorescents and Pastels

Color Theory

Color theory is the collection of rules and guidelines which designers use to communicate with users through appealing color schemes in visual interfaces. To pick the best colors every time, designers use a color wheel and refer to extensive collected knowledge about human optical ability, psychology, culture and more.
